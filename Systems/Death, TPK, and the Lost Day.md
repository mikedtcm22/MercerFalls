# Death, TPK, and the Lost Day

Up: [[Game Systems]]

**In the dungeon, "death" is hollowing, not death (see [[Hollowing and Agency-Loss]]). The [[The Embers|ember]] intervenes, the body dissolves and walks home on autopilot, and the cost lands as *lost agency* — narrated at the table with the player watching. Outside the dungeon, death is permanent. And time itself runs differently below: *how* you leave decides what the delve costs you.**

## How time works in the dungeon — the two currencies
Time doesn't move normally underground, and the exit condition sets the bill:

- **Leave of your own free will** — a normal exit *or* riding one of [[Wesley's Sigils|Wesley's portals]] (the portal is a shortcut through *space*, not time) — and the dungeon spits you onto the [[The Mill|mill]] grounds a **fixed dwell of 3 [[Time Points]]** after you entered, no matter how long you subjectively spent, with **no partial-delve refund** (leave "early" and it still costs 3). You pay in **time.**
- **Get hollowed** and you're returned as if **almost no world-time passed** — you reconstitute at the entrance essentially at the moment you dropped. You pay **nothing in time**, and everything in **agency:** you lose control of your character for the **rest of that day and all of the next** (the lost day, below).

That asymmetry is the whole lesson. Escaping looks *expensive* (3 TP, and maybe a late night); hollowing looks *free* (0 TP, instant) — and the free one is the trap.

### The 3-TP dwell, in play
- **Entering costs 1 TP** — the floor. As long as a kid has at least one Time Point, they can always go in.
- **A successful free-willed delve costs 3 TP total,** drawn from wherever they have them (day pool, then night).
- **Deficit scales the consequence.** Enter with 3+ in hand and you surface with runway to spare — home at a reasonable hour. Enter short and the delve still bills 3, so you surface **unexpectedly late** by however far underwater you were: a little over, a little late; way over, out till dawn.
- **Night delves aren't a special rule** — just "entered without a 3-TP runway, so you surface at first light." (Exactly why the [[Session 1 — The Dare|dare]] survivors wake on the grounds at dawn.)
- **The rules bend in the fiction, always with a trade-off.** A coordinated sleepover cover can buy a no-consequence night delve — until a parent compares notes. Whether a family expects a check-in *between* the day and night blocks is a per-kid, per-episode variable (a planned family dinner can make a "late" return costly even before 9 p.m.). See [[Time Points]] for the domestic layer.

## What costs what

| Outcome | Cost |
|---|---|
| Survive a delve (free-willed exit) | **3 TP** (the dwell); surface late if you entered with fewer |
| One+ PCs hollowed, others survive | Survivors pay the 3 TP; the hollowed pay **0 TP** but lose control of the **rest of today + all of tomorrow** |
| TPK (all hollowed) | All pay 0 TP; all lose **rest of today + tomorrow** to autopilot |
| **Die outside the dungeon** | **Permanent death** |

("Die" is genre shorthand; the underlying event is hollowing.)

## The dissolve moment

When a PC drops, their ember intervenes visibly — fire vocabulary throughout. The warmth in their chest *catches*, flares, light pours upward and disperses; the body becomes translucent, dissolves into motes that drift to the ceiling and vanish; a faint shimmer (residue) lingers on the floor and fades over minutes.

> [!danger] DM-only — what the dissolve actually is
> The light is [[The Entity|the entity]]'s substance being spent. The upward motes are the PC's *agency* being routed to the dungeon. The shimmer is residue, and an ember-bearer leaves only a faint trace because the ember absorbs nearly the whole transaction (see [[Forensic Delving]]). Each PC hollowing also ticks the hidden **Surfeit** counter (see [[The Embers]] and [[Wesley's Sigils]]).

**Don't pause the encounter.** The other PCs see the warmth catch and the dissolve, and have to keep fighting — they process while losing.

## Reconstitution and the walk home

The hollowed PC's body re-coalesces at the dungeon's entrance — same clothes, same gear, glassy-eyed, silent, unresponsive to its own name; the warmth in their chest is *cold ash, banked too long*. This happens as if **almost no time has passed in the world** (the hollowed pay in agency, not time). The body walks home on autopilot — route-correct, climbing fences, ignoring traffic, weather, and people — lets itself in, climbs into bed, sleeps.

Because the hollowed surface "instantly" while free-willed survivors are held the full 3-TP dwell, **the two groups don't cross paths at the mill** — survivors are still below when the hollowed walk home. (In the pilot: the hollowed autopilot home in the dead of night; survivors don't surface until dawn.)

**The dungeon walk of shame** is *witnessable* — by townspeople (the [[The Reality Filter|Filter]] handles civilians: they perceive the strangeness, don't follow up, rationalize it by morning) and, crucially, by other ember-bearers and PCs who didn't delve and are along the route. **PCs and ember-bearers are not Filtered — they remember.** Staking out [[The Mill]] or a route home lets PCs witness adherent walks, recruit walks, and eventually each other's — a major forensic channel that doesn't require entering the dungeon (pairs with [[Forensic Delving]]).

## What a lost day looks like

The hollowed PC's body, after reconstituting, spends the **remainder of that day and the whole next day** on **auto-pilot** — flat affect, default behaviors, *present without will.* They wake in their own bed with no memory of the walk home or of the lost day; the body is intact, the world is normal, and the Filter covered the late return with the parents. They show up to scheduled things and say almost nothing; they don't push, ask, or insist. Anything time-locked (Wesley's countdown, NPC plotlines) advances unimpeded.

> [!note] What they *do* and don't forget
> They keep everything up to the moment they dropped — the descent, the fight, the warmth. They lose only the reconstitution/walk home and the lost day itself. So they know they went in and came out changed; they just can't account for how they got home or where the day went. (Keeps the party's shared "we all have this now" hook intact.)

## How a lost day plays at the table

Lost days are **not skipped — they are narrated, with the player watching.** This is the campaign's most teeth-bearing dramatic lever. **Never do anything nefarious with the puppeted character — just mundane, useless things** (chores, TV, a shrug where help was needed) while the others spend their Time Points on things that matter. The point is the felt loss of agency, not humiliation.

> [!note] The first instance — calibrate to the situation
> The lever lands hardest in a **mixed** outcome, where the contrast teaches itself (my buddy is investigating; I'm watching the DM make me rake leaves). On a **full TPK** — very possible in the pilot's buzzsaw room — there's no one to contrast against and no plot yet to lose, so don't puppet four boring days at four brand-new players: compress to the wordless dawn montage and save the full lever for the first *later* hollowing, when it has teeth. See [[Session 1 — The Dare]].

- **Single PC death:** the DM narrates the dead PC's day in real time alongside the survivors. When the dead PC's "turn" comes, they do — pointedly — nothing: a shrug, a flat half-smile, a reasonable-sounding deflection where intervention was needed. The dead player stays at the table as **pure witness** — they don't get to say "my character wouldn't," because the horror is that today their character does. Aim for **one or two stinging beats**, not a play-by-play.
- **TPK:** a compressed montage — three or four images that sting most (the rival party moving uncontested, an NPC plotline resolving badly, a small broken promise), then cut to the morning. On a TPK, all four kids reconstitute at the mill, possibly together, and walk off in different directions in the dawn light — the campaign's signature image.
- **Partial wipe — temporally fractured party:** survivors have agency tomorrow; the dead don't. Survivors can investigate, confront a cultist, warn someone, sit on their hands, or run a short-handed delve — while the dead PC watches their own body move through the day not acting.

## What persists through hollowing / TPK

- XP earned before the killing blow (kept)
- Loot found before the killing blow (kept)
- Map knowledge, glyph translations, NPC info (kept — see the players' Atlas notes in [[The Keystone Tablet]])
- The [[The Keystone Tablet]] and any acquired keystones (kept — bonded to bearer + entity, not the dungeon)
- Spell slots reset (effective long rest); HP full
- Consumables spent during the run are gone

The daily reset (see [[Floor Reset and the Intelligence Gradient]]) operates independently — it never compounds on a TPK; every morning is a fresh dungeon.
