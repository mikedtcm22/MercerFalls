# Combatscape Layers

Up: [[Game Systems]]

**A room where a fight can happen should never be a bare box of grid squares. Build combat-capable rooms in four stacked layers, so that when a wandering monster (or a planned encounter) lands, the *room* is half the fight.**

This applies to any room where combat is likely — and because [[Dungeon Mechanics|wandering-monster]] rolls can drop a fight into almost any room, that's most of them. Design the space so position and interaction matter as much as hit points.

## The four layers
- **Layer 0 — Base map.** The raw geography: the room's shape and size. Irregular footprints beat rectangles.
- **Layer 1 — Movement.** Routes, hazards, and obstacles that force choices from turn one — winding lanes instead of a straight charge, difficult terrain, a gap or drop, a chokepoint. Decisions start here.
- **Layer 2 — Position.** Spots worth fighting *for*: high ground, cover, chokepoints, a perch with sightlines. These become goals on the map, not just scenery.
- **Layer 3 — Interaction.** Destructibles and objects combatants can use, move, trigger, or turn against foes — a suspended load to drop, barrels to topple or ignite, a cart to shove, weak flooring to lure someone onto. This makes the map *reactive*.

## Use notes
- Not every room needs all four, but a combat-likely room should have **at least Layer 1 plus one strong Layer-3 interactable** — enough that a clever party can turn a fight with the environment.
- The interactables double as teaching: the party's **first fight** should hand them an obvious "use the room" option so they learn to look for it (see the first encounter in [[The Expedition Post]]).
- Pairs with the [[Dungeon Mechanics|Stretch-Worth Principle]] (an interactable is often also the "reason to spend a Stretch") and with Floor 1's fixed-layout memory reward (learn a room, exploit it faster next time).
