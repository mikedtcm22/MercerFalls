# Narration & Flavor Style

The governing rule for read-aloud and flavor text at the table.

## Locations vs. moments

- **Locations — describe, don't perform.** When flavor text introduces a space (a room, a building, the grounds the PCs just walked into), the language should be **descriptive and concrete**: help the players *visualize what they see* — layout, objects, state of decay, where things are. Avoid cinematic/literary "flowery" prose. Let a location's unease come from concrete, frozen-in-place detail (a coffee mug left on a sill, a hard hat faded white, dust over a workbench) rather than from *telling* the players it's creepy.
- **Moments — let it land.** Reserve dramatic flair for **events, creatures, reveals, and scripted beats**: a cold open, a monster's first appearance, the surge of acquiring a relic, a hollowing. That's where heightened, literary language earns its place.

Rule of thumb: if the players need to *navigate or search* the thing, make it legible; if they need to *feel* the thing, make it land.

## Worked examples (DM dashboard)

- *Descriptive (location):* the blast-furnace interior as a clear spatial layout — furnace at center, catwalks overhead, the channeled river below, foreman's office and lockers along the wall — so the search reads cleanly; the mill grounds as a gravel yard, intact buildings, and specific abandoned objects.
- *Flair (moment):* the Session-0 cold open; the creature stirring in the yard; the Surge → Settle of the Awakening.

## Related narration disciplines (accrete future style calls here)

- **Embers are never named at the table.** Player-facing language uses fire/warmth vocabulary only — coals, a banked warmth in the chest, something that catches and won't go out. See [[The Embers]].
- **Cold-open tradition.** Each session opens with a short mood-setting overture. The standard form is *away from the PCs, on the episode's threat* (Supernatural-style); the exception is the campaign's first cold open, which is PC-facing. See [[Session 1 — The Dare]] and [[Session 0]].
- **Real first, wrong second.** Restraint over shock — ground a scene in the ordinary before the wrongness arrives, and keep every early tell deniable.
