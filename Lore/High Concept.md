# High Concept

Up: [[Lore & Cosmology]]

**The Satanic Panic was right, but also wrong: there is a real hidden infernal machine under Mercer Falls, and it eats agency. The kids who stumble into it become the only people who can see what's happening *and act on it*.**

## The Pitch (player-facing)

Mercer Falls, Ohio, summer 1996. A rust-belt town that feels stuck in a way economic decline alone doesn't explain. Beneath it runs a century-old machine — see [[The Dungeon]] — that consumes the human capacity to act. Time-loss is how that consumption looks from outside; the underlying fact is the machine has eaten the part of a person that *picks* (see [[Hollowing and Agency-Loss]]).

The PCs are kids who fall into this in the last weeks of summer break. Through circumstance, not destiny, they alone can perceive and intervene. They are protected by [[The Embers]] — fragments of cosmic substance lodged in their chests that grant their D&D abilities and dampen the machine's toll.

Tonal touchstones: *Buffy the Vampire Slayer*, *Stranger Things* (deliberately diverged), *Persona*. Scope: Levels 1–5 as a self-contained arc (the Mill-sector crawl, the long summer, the autumn-equinox climax), extensible through 6–20.

> [!danger] DM-only — the hidden frame
> The dungeon is a **containment system** built by cosmic-scale **Order** to suppress cosmic-scale **Chaos** (see [[Order and Chaos]]). Order's "solution" was to drain choice out of the surrounding population, because choice is what makes Chaos. The PCs' benevolent patron — the source of the embers — is in fact the contained Chaos itself (see [[The Entity]]). Their level-20 patron is the BBEG. They will not learn this for a long time.

## The Themes

The campaign's deepest argument: **agency is the answer.** Both cosmic forces want existence cleansed of choice — Order by suppression, Chaos by destruction. The third position the PCs must articulate and embody is that the capacity to choose is exactly what makes life worth living, and protecting it is the actual work, against any cosmic force that promises to relieve you of it.

## Structural Note: Failure Does Not End the Campaign

If the PCs fail an objective, the town gets worse instead of better and the next thing happens. Every important objective has a playable success-downstream and failure-downstream. The Act 1 fork (Rival Party vs. Evil Party) is the canonical example — see [[The Countdown Structure]] and [[Wesley Crane]].

## Language Discipline

The player-facing register never names the embers directly. Players experience their relic only in fire vocabulary — an internal warmth, a small persistent fire in the chest. The title's connection to what they carry is designed to land as a *meta-realization* in late Act 1 or beyond, never as a stated fact at character creation. See [[The Embers]].
