# The Cast and the Permanent Cast

Up: [[Lore & Cosmology]]

**The [[Order and Chaos|Order]] error stated at human scale — and the generator for every [[Wesley's Recruits|Rival]]'s pressure-point and saving-grace.**

> [!danger] DM-only
> A design principle and a piece of cosmology. Never stated at the table; it's the shape underneath [[Wesley Crane]]'s recruiting and the PCs' counter to it.

## The principle
A temporary surrender of agency is real, good, and sometimes necessary — letting someone carry you while you can't carry yourself, like a **cast on a broken leg.** It is healthy *because it ends:* the cast exists so the leg can heal and the person can walk on their own again. The error — Wesley's, and Order's — is mistaking the cast for the cure, and making it **permanent, total, whole-body, forever.** *Help that refuses to end is control wearing help's face.*

This is the [[Order and Chaos|Order]] jaw at human scale. Order isn't "structure is bad" — structure, support, a held boundary are all good *in service of agency returning.* Order's sin is the cast that never comes off. (Chaos's mirror error: **no cast at all** — *"you don't need support, just be free"* — abandoning the person to a leg that can't yet bear weight. Both deny that the whole point is *walking on your own.*)

## Wesley's version
[[Wesley Crane]] is **sometimes right — about the cast, never about its duration.** His genuine successes were people given room to take their own wheel back; he misreads them as proof that *him taking the wheel* is the cure. His "failures" are often people he wouldn't let *out* of the cast. He holds a true thing by the wrong end — and never knows it. (His Phase-2 flip is the same error scaled to apocalypse: cast → **amputation** — break the whole structure of choice. His Act-3 vulnerability is the memory he can't face: someone he "saved" who got better and *left*, proving the cast was always meant to come off.)

## The Rival generator (pressure-point ↔ saving-grace)
Every [[Wesley's Recruits|Rival]] is built as a matched pair off this principle:
- **Pressure-point** (Wesley's lever) = the place the kid secretly agrees *"I can't trust myself; just tell me what to do"* — their specific **hand-over-the-wheel** moment. A wound that reads (to them, and to Wesley) as *"you keep choosing this."*
- **Saving-grace** (the party's counter) = the kid's own buried agency — the **domain where they actually can be trusted to walk**, if someone believes it. The party can't out-care Wesley; their one structural advantage is **help aimed at the kid not needing them** — peer-trust, treating them as an agent, knowing when the cast comes off.
- **The risk stays real.** Trusting a not-quite-healed leg can mean watching it buckle; sometimes a kid trusted with the wheel crashes the car. That's why Wesley's pitch is seductive and the party's bet is brave, not safe — and why the [[Aftermath Branches|equinox outcome]] is genuinely in play.

This also rhymes with the PCs' own arcs (each carrying or fleeing an imposed Order), and plugs directly into the disposition mechanics — see [[The Negotiation System]].
