# The Nightmare Engine

Up: [[Lore & Cosmology]]

**The dungeon does not invent its monsters. It curates the nightmares already in the air and gives them bodies.**

> *The dungeon does not invent. It curates.*

## How It Works

Every creature [[The Dungeon]] spawns traces back to a human imagination — a nightmare, a campfire story, a horror novel, a comic, a B-movie, a child's sketch, an arcade boss, a piece of urban folklore. The dungeon harvests these images from the surrounding cultural ecosystem and assembles bodies from them, instantiated by its Order-aligned mechanism, given physical form and territorial behavior, and bound by the daily reset. The monsters are **made here**, from local raw material — not summoned, not visiting.

This is a clean expression of the cosmology (see [[Order and Chaos]]): creativity is Chaos-aligned, and the dungeon is a **filing system for fear** — it takes Chaos's profusion of dreamings and imposes structure (these limbs, this hunting pattern, this vulnerability). The PCs' [[The Embers|embers]] sit uneasily here: they grant small impossible abilities the structuring logic can't quite catch, so **ember-channeled solutions can occasionally kill monsters in ways the source didn't anticipate**. The canonical vulnerability is the *most reliable* way, not the *only* way.

## Locality and the Cultural Catchment

The dungeon draws on a roughly bounded catchment centered on its location — the school, the library, the comic shop, the diner, the kids' three-a.m. dreams. Its bestiary reflects the **taste of its territory**: Universal monsters from late-night TV, Stephen King paperbacks, slasher rentals, Goosebumps from the elementary library, comic villains, Genesis/arcade bosses, urban legends about [[The Mercer Mansion]], hunters' woods-ghost-stories. The bestiary *breathes with the town* — a new film at the local cinema adds creatures within weeks; a faded fear drops out. Monsters from sources outside the catchment cannot appear.

## Player Investigation Mechanic

Once the PCs encounter a monster, they identify it and learn weaknesses by consulting cultural sources — treat as a research scene (see [[Forensic Delving]]). Approaches:

- **Recognition.** Personal familiarity → recognition on sight or low-DC Int check. Be generous.
- **Pop-culture research.** Visit [[Panels]] ([[Russ]]), the public/school library, the video store, a genre-obsessed friend → successful Investigation pulls the source, often with the vulnerability stated outright.
- **Folklore research.** Older/archetypal creatures → local history, [[Mercer Falls Historical Society]], retired teachers, the diner old-timer. Less precise, more atmospheric.
- **Cross-checking.** Sources disagree → most widely-circulated or most recent canonical version generally wins.

Research returns **concrete, actionable intel** — name, behavior, vulnerability — not vague hints. The challenge is in *applying* the intel, not extracting it. **Pace note:** keep it fast — one check and a paragraph of read-aloud, not a montage.

## Russ as Research Hub

[[Russ]] at [[Panels]] is the PCs' primary research contact — he has spent his life cataloguing exactly the horror media the dungeon draws from. He doesn't yet know his collection is weaponizable; the PCs teach him over the campaign. He recognizes creatures with high reliability from any specific description, and lends tapes, comics, paperbacks, games. His Filter-touched-not-yet-broken status: see [[The Reality Filter]] and his note.

## DM Craft: Designing a Monster Under This Rule

Work backwards from a cultural source, not forwards from mechanics. For each monster: pick a **primary source** (write it in notes); define the **stated vulnerability** (the canonical kill condition); define **one secondary tell** (a sound, smell, visual, tic) bridging encounter to research; **plant the source** somewhere reachable (Russ's shop, a library, a kid at school, late-night TV, a bedroom poster), ideally via more than one route. Invented sources are fine — pick evocative titles. Monster *strength* is not governed by this rule; calibrate to the encounter as normal.

**Reskinning existing stat blocks (the default move).** The fastest way to build under this rule is often to run it *backwards*: take a mechanically convenient D&D stat block and dress it as a local nightmare, keeping the numbers and swapping the skin. A pack of gnolls becomes the **Dogmen** of Midwest campfire legend; a bugbear becomes the thing that lives under the overpass. The stat block is invisible to the players — only the skin, the secondary tell, and the source they research are real to them. This is the campaign's standing habit: **D&D mechanics, Mercer Falls flavor.** (Mundane weapons and armor are the one exception — those stay D&D-flavored; see [[Gear and Items]].)

## Floor Lair Themes

Each dungeon floor carries a **loose lair theme** — one creature type (occasionally two) that has claimed the floor as territory — layered with ambient wanderers that don't belong to the theme. The theme gives a floor a legible identity ("this is the pack floor," "the drowned floor"), ties its rooms together, and lets returning players build territorial intuition. It should fit both the cultural catchment (above) and the floor's tone, and it sets the spine of that floor's wandering table; ambient wanderers and the occasional apex round it out (see [[Dungeon Mechanics]], Encounter-Type Discipline, for how the table is rolled).

**Floor 1 — "the pack floor."** Territory theme: **pack predators.** The organized core are the **Dogmen** (reskinned gnolls — rank-and-file Gnoll, CR 1/2; a Gnoll Pack Lord, CR 2, as alpha), holding the floor as a hunting range. Around them roam unaligned feral beasts: some weaker than the dogmen (feral strays, Giant Hyena, CR 1), some stronger — that even the dogmen give ground to (candidates: dire wolves / worgs, death dogs CR 1; the exact "thing they fear" TBD). The Pit Room's (9) sleeping wolves belong to this theme, as do the two dead dogmen in the [[The Octagonal Vestibule|Vestibule]] (Wesley's pre-clear). Register: **beast, not heavy-supernatural** — deliberately pitched below [[The Hush]] (Episode 1's curated apex, which sits atop this food chain). Full wandering table and stat spread: build with the Floor-1 bestiary. See [[The Mill Sector]].

## Edge Cases

- **Can't spawn what no one imagined.** Bounded by Mercer Falls' cultural exposure.
- **Belief, not accuracy, drives instantiation.** If locals believe silver kills werewolves, the dungeon's werewolves die to silver — even if the source said otherwise. Remakes can overwrite older versions.
- **PCs can seed the bestiary, in principle.** Intense, consistent nightmares could eventually contribute a creature. Rare, slow, an Act 2/3 concern.
- **Some entities don't follow this rule.** The deepest-floor creatures — particularly past [[The Mercer Gate]] — are **not curated**, not from human culture, with no source, no vulnerability, and no name a human ever gave. The campaign's late-game cosmic-horror payoff: the filing system has limits.
- **Improvised solutions can work.** Lean yes-and — accept the invocation (retroactively establishing the source) or rule the creature is from a source where it doesn't apply.

## Worked Example: The Hush

[[The Hush]] (Episode 1's antagonist) is drawn from a B-movie about an eyeless predator that hunts by sound. Russ can ID it on sight from a description. Canonical vulnerability: **total silence in a confined space** (straight from the film's climax) — the intel the PCs walk out of Panels with. The Hush's *juvenile* (in the Pillared Reach, with a spore-tracking mechanic tying the descent to the topside plot) is the dungeon's permitted **extrapolation** from the source, not a feature of it. See [[Episode 1 — The Hush]].
