# Tank

Barbarian · the popular multi-sport jock (football, wrestling, baseball — anything highly physical). Lives in [[The Districts|the Flats]]. Outwardly nonchalant and wild; secretly **incredibly disciplined**. Ideal: *strength is for the people who can't carry their own* (protector). Flaw: *he measures himself only by what he can carry, and hides the kid who might want something just for himself* — so well that people love the performance, or the achievement, but never the actual boy.

## The read — two masters, neither of them him
"Big dumb jock" on the outside, immense *self-imposed* [[Order and Chaos|Order]] underneath — the 5 a.m. workouts, last to leave practice, the discipline no one sees; the wild-jock act is the mask over the machine. But the deepest thing he hides isn't the talent (everyone sees that) or even the discipline — it's **how much he *cares*.** Caring that much, wanting it that badly, is uncool, so he keeps the *wanting* deniable.

The core: **Tank has made his wanting into a service to others so thoroughly he can no longer tell whether he wants to be great for *himself*.** Admitting it's his own want is more terrifying than any amount of work — because if it's *his*, then failing at it is his too, and there's no one he "did it for." So he launders the want through two audiences:
- **To the team**, he *hides* the caring — plays it off as effortless gift, not need (caring is uncool).
- **To his dad**, he *performs* the caring as duty — the dutiful son carrying the dream, so it reads as obligation, not desire.
Neither audience ever sees a boy who simply wants the thing *for himself*. As he framed it: **his teammates want him to win; his dad wants him to be the best** — and both of those are still someone else's want. (Win = lean on raw talent, the tried-and-true play, low risk. Best = improvement, which means trying in the open and eating visible failure. But both are *for the eyes on him*.)

- **The engine — lonely at the top.** Owning the want fully would put him in a class by himself, and **he doesn't want to be alone**, so he keeps the want deniable to stay in company. The fear isn't failure exactly; it's that *wanting it for himself is isolating*, so he routes the want through people who'll keep him company in it.
- **The load-bearing detail — his father.** A [[The 1991 Mill Incident|mill worker]] who never recovered from the closing and has relocated all his unspent hope onto Tank's body — Tank as "the only way out." [[Order and Chaos|Order]] in its most intimate, sympathetic form: a cage built of love and desperation, hardest to resist because resisting feels like betrayal.
- **The two jaws.** *Order:* the dad-dream — be the best, carry the family's hope; the deepest seduction is anything that promises to **make Dad okay** (he'd walk into real wrongness for it, because it launders as love). *Chaos:* the locker-room dream of **pure release** — drop the weight, indulge the wildness he only performs; but "release" isn't agency either, it's dumping the weight onto his dad and calling abandonment freedom.
- **The third position.** Tell a weight he *chose* from a weight that was *put on him*. Not abandon his dad (Chaos) and not be the mule forever (Order), but **claim a want of his own first** — and then, if he still chooses to help his father, do it as his decision, bounded, on his terms. The discipline (genuinely his, genuinely admirable) becomes *his* the moment the goal does. Resolution (locked direction, rhyming with [[Gern]]): **he keeps the achieving-athlete life — but now because *he* wants it, owned and exposed,** not as the family's assignment or the crew's effortless act.
- **Tank and [[Old Man]] are the same wound from opposite social positions** — both load-bearing kids carrying a parent's collapse (Old Man's invisible/indoor; Tank's public/physical). Run the *"your suffering is load-bearing"* lie against both; later, let them recognize each other.
- **Craft note:** protect the reveal — let the table (and the players) be fooled by the dumb-jock act for a while, until a quiet scene shows the 5 a.m. kid. Don't make him transparently Secretly Deep from session one.

## Awakening test — "The Pep Rally" (locked)
The room is the **school gym**, dressed for tomorrow's pep rally — posters laid out on the floor overnight. Two exits, each barred by a gate: one opens onto the electric **stadium** (game-night roar, the thrill of the sport); the other onto a large, beautiful, **empty banquet hall** with a **throne on a high dais** visible through it. Two performance-stations, each a thing the room *watches him do* (not a control he operates):
- **Friends-station — the trick shot.** An easy flashy shot at a hoop/target, the casual flex. Easy check; on a miss **the ball snaps back into his hand before it can resolve** — the room won't *let* the flex fail (an eerie property of the space, not a dice rescue — consistent with a chamber that *prepared itself for him*). Its exposed gearing runs visibly toward the **stadium** gate.
- **Dad-station — the power-clean.** A loaded barbell he must clean off the floor and rack on a wall catch. Tough Athletics check; **failure is allowed** — he resets and grinds until it's done. Its exposed gearing runs visibly toward the **throne** gate.

**The posters (carried diegetically, found on the floor):**
> *(center / preamble)* **Big day today, Tank. / They want a show. He wants a champion. / Go give somebody what they came for.**
>
> *(friends-station)* **They love how easy you make it look. / Never let them see it cost you.**
>
> *(dad-station)* **They're counting on you to be the best. / Never let them down.**

A player reads it cleanly going in: flick the easy shot for the stadium, or grind the lift for the throne. He performs one. **The twist:** whichever station he works, the **same gate lifts — the throne**; the stadium gate never moves. The exposed gearing lets him catch, a half-second early, that **both chains always fed the throne axle** (discoverable, not a GM cheat — the room reveals its true shape). On the dais the ember manifests as a **crown**; he picks it up and it resonates as his. Then Tank himself dissolves from the scene with his ember. See [[The Ember Chamber]], [[The Embers]].

**Staging:** from the throne the **stadium stays audible and visible through its still-barred gate** — he ends up enthroned, elevated, looking at the warmth he never reached. The two doors marry: "be the best / rise above" cashes out as a lonely seat overlooking the crowd he can hear but not join.

**The asymmetry that *is* the characterization:** the dad-station permits visible, repeated failure (and makes him earn it); the friends-station *forbids* failure (the flex can never be seen to miss). One path lets the effort show, one path's whole premise is *don't let them see you try*. The room encodes his evasion in his hands, no narration needed.

**The tell (retrospective). [[The Entity|Chaos]] doesn't care which station** — to it they're the same move: performance aimed at an audience, agency outsourced. The ember rewards the *doing*, blind to *whose goal he was doing it for* — the exact blindness he has to overcome. The **absent third station** — *do it because he wants it, with no one to play to* — is the option the machine structurally can't contain, and the inscriptions never offer (every line is an imperative to perform for someone). No redemption is rehearsed here; the test just makes the trap visible. *(Held latent: a crown is what the dad-dream looks like cashed out — elevated, admired, alone; the campaign's reclaim-the-path turn is whether Tank ever reaches a greatness that isn't a crown on an empty dais.)*

## Battle plan
- **Through-line — "Who Was That For?"** Tank's temptation arrives not as an object ([[Bash Whitlock|Bash]]'s gear), his own voice ([[Old Man]]'s), or belonging-on-offer ([[Gern]]'s) — it arrives as **the crowd's gaze**: the constant availability of an audience to perform for, which lets him never own a want as his. The DM's quiet recurring question behind every impressive Tank beat is *who was that for?* — and the answer is always the team (hide the caring) or his dad (perform the caring as duty), never "me, because I love it." Keep making *that* honest answer the one that's neither cool nor dutiful, so he won't say it.
- **Early/mid lean — the kid-sized mirror (no cosmology needed).** Run the two-audiences split early. Give him chances to be impressive and let him reflexively **style it off for the crew** ("wasn't even trying") — reward the cool, then show the cost: the people clapping don't know him; the act keeps everyone at arm's length. Separately, surface the **dad-pressure** (a phone call, an expectation, a coach/scout, the family's hope) and let him grind dutifully, framed as "for Dad" — so even his most genuine effort gets laundered into obligation. Then plant the **contrast beat**: a moment where doing the thing purely for love (no crowd, no dad, just him) is on the table, and watch whether he can let the wanting show. His first taste of the third door is the first time he goes all-in, *owned*, and someone responds to **that** version of him. (Honor the craft note — keep the 5 a.m. kid hidden until a quiet scene exposes him.)
- **Crisis & turn.** Both jaws escalate. *Order's* deepest play: something that promises to **make Dad okay** — Tank would walk into real wrongness for it, because it launders as love (the campaign's wrongness offering to fix/secure his dad's situation). *Chaos's* play, and his potential **destructive-rebellion detour** (the rhyme with Gern): torch the discipline, blow off the dream, live the wild persona *for real* — abandonment dressed as freedom. The crisis forces the question the test couldn't: not "dad or team" but **"what do *you* want, and can you bear that it's yours?"** The turn is Tank **claiming a want as his own and bearing the loneliness and exposure of it** — at which the discipline becomes genuinely his, the dad-relationship becomes a thing he *chooses* (bounded, his terms) rather than carries, and — rhyming with Gern — the people who connect with the all-in, owned version are the connection the deniable version forfeited.
- **Reserve assets.**
  - **His father** — the **Order** lever, the hardest and most intimate; aim it via anything that promises to make Dad okay. (Name/details TBD.)
  - **The crew / the cool-guy audience** — the everyday venue for hiding the caring, and the social cost of being seen to try.
  - **[[Dominique Ferraro]]** — his crush, and the person whose regard he might want *for himself* rather than as performance; a natural lever for the "seen for real" beat (and the live [[Bash Whitlock|Bash]] connection).
  - **[[Old Man]]** — the twin; the mutual-recognition payoff, and the shared *"your suffering is load-bearing"* lie to run against both.
  - **A specific arena** (a big game, tryout, scout, record attempt) — a structural stage for "who was that for," and the moment he could do it all-in for himself.
  - **The lonely-at-the-top instinct** — the engine; escalate situations where excelling isolates him, forcing the choice between deniable-belonging and owned-greatness.

## Foils
- **Tank ↔ [[Gern]] (the locked rhyme).** Both hide a *caring* to keep a warm crowd — Gern his conviction, Tank his wanting — and both must reclaim an imposed path as a *chosen* one. Their Awakening tests are deliberate mirror topologies: Gern's is a **divergence** (one symbol, two fates); Tank's is a **convergence** (two performances, one fate). Different machine, same lesson.
- **Tank ↔ [[Old Man]].** The same wound (a load-bearing kid holding up a collapsed parent), opposite social positions; the same lie; an eventual mutual recognition already wired in.

## At the mill / relationships
Tank came to the mill on a **prank**, not a dare — to impress his jock crew (proposed by [[Ricky Vance]]); the goal was to scare [[Bash Whitlock]] and [[Gern]] into leaving within an hour. He has *power* in the dynamic and misuses it mildly — not from cruelty (he isn't cruel) but from the same need everyone has: to belong. The gap between his public performance and private decency shows immediately, and his guilt becomes a redemptive lever once the prank turns into mortal danger. Fellow [[The Districts|Flats]] kid with [[Old Man]] (same street). Connected to [[Bash Whitlock]] through [[Dominique Ferraro]] — Tank's **crush** on her, which Bash leverages (recruiting him as a protector and to phreak a free second phone line). Crewmate of [[Ricky Vance]]. See [[The Party]], [[Session 1 — The Dare]].
