# Episode 2 — The Hush

Up: [[Episodes]]

**A standalone Monster-of-the-Week: the dare-night creature comes home. This is where [[Time Points|Town-Time]] is introduced, where the town starts blaming the PCs for [[Ricky Vance|Ricky]]'s disappearance, and where the heroic climax is a night rescue — pulling Ricky out of the [[The Hush|Hush]]'s den before he's "ripe." Featured Rival: [[Ricky Vance]] (disposition 2 — Neutral, shaped by the rescue). Wesley fingerprint level: none — the Hush was already loose.**

## The spine (six pillars)

1. **[[Dominique Ferraro|Dominique]] as the connector.** The warm through-line who pulls a loose, not-yet-bonded party back together — worried for her cousin Bash, furious at Tank (see *Reassembly*, below). Unambiguously good news here, and secretly the first turn of the "woman in the van" betrayal (per [[Dominique Ferraro]], every step of her drift must *look like* good news; this episode is where the players fall for Free-Dominique).
2. **The PCs risk getting blamed for Ricky's disappearance.** [[Gern]] is the named scapegoat; all four are tarred as "the mill kids." Engine and fiction in *Why the PCs get blamed*, below. First sustained social-pressure beat; a personal, no-supernatural-buy-in reason to spend Time Points.
3. **[[Tank]] is stalked by the Hush at night** — the pheromone-marked voice from the Session-1 juvenile. [[Old Man]] surfaces the monster-in-town idea *first* (see *Reassembly*); the mark is the supernatural thread that converges on the den. See *The mark*.
4. **Exploration reveals the lore that builds the clock and the world.** The discoverable ripening window (*Ricky's clock*, below); the [[The Stone of Quietude|stone of quietude]] lead (paying off the Session-1 Marv's-Spot photo); the [[The Library|Library]]'s disappearance pattern; and a first, un-explained taste of [[The Reality Filter]] via adults being *oddly accepting* (see *The Filter, introduced by texture*). Full node-by-node breakdown in [[Episode 2 — The Hush — Town-Time Investigation Map]].
5. **A delve is on the table** — an optional second dungeon run for the stone of quietude, which makes the climax materially easier but is never required. Ties to the Marv's-Spot snapshot if a PC pocketed it in [[Session 1 — The Dare|Session 1]]. (Location deliberately off Wesley's Session-1 trail — see [[The Stone of Quietude]] / [[The Keystone Tablet]].)
6. **The climax brings the kids to the woods at night**, where they run into Ricky's father — [[Wade Vance]] — already hunting the thing, and he helps them rescue Ricky. He may get hurt (badly) depending on how the operation goes; that outcome sets Ricky's disposition. See *The climax* and *Ricky's disposition outcomes*.

## The shape of the session
**The opening is staged out of order to honor the Episode-1 split** (see *Reassembly*): cold open (Ricky's capture, his POV — the other side of the receding clicks) → **Day B**: [[Tank]] wakes to [[Dominique Ferraro|Dominique]]'s confrontation, [[Old Man]] to the neighborhood kid (the monster in the streets + the lost day) → **Day A (flashback, "the day before")**: [[Bash Whitlock|Bash]] & [[Gern]]'s first Time-Points, the accusation, and crossing paths with placid, hollowed Tank & Old Man → **Day-B reconvene** (four vectors home) → daytime/nighttime investigation (the two tracks) → optional stone-of-quietude delve → night: the woods, the den, Wade, the rescue-fight → aftermath + Ricky's disposition lands.

The investigation is built as **two tracks that converge**: Track A ("Where's Ricky?" — social/blame, daylight) and Track B ("What's hunting me?" — Tank's stalk, after dark). The turn of the episode is the PCs connecting them — *the thing hunting Tank is what took Ricky* — at which point the clock goes urgent and the den becomes the shared target. See the map note.

## Explanatory notes (the "why" behind the pieces)

### Why the PCs get blamed
Ricky's bully clique needs a story for why he vanished, and they build the cruelest available one: **"You snitched to Ricky's dad about the dare — that's why he got shipped off to a military-style camp for the rest of the summer."** It sticks for a structural reason — **both Ricky *and* his father are missing** (Wade is off hunting the Hush; see *The climax*), which reads to the town as *"his dad took him away,"* confirming the fiction. The scapegoat can't disprove it without either revealing a truth he doesn't have or sounding insane.
- **The named scapegoat is [[Gern]]** — the most painful, on-theme target: he took the dare at face value, never knew it was a prank, and being frozen out the one summer he's finally getting "new eyes" is his kid-sized mirror with the stakes cranked. No protector, easiest target. The smear still covers "those mill kids," so [[Dominique Ferraro|Dominique]] intervenes for her cousin [[Bash Whitlock|Bash]] anyway (hard-wiring the reassembly).
- **The party crucible (deferred to the reconvene):** [[Tank]] ran the prank and [[Old Man]] clocked it — but on **Day A both are hollowed and can't help,** so Gern faces the clique *alone* (which is exactly what makes him reach for the others). Whether the party closes ranks around him lands at the **Day-B reconvene** — the first real bonding test, and the first turn of Tank's guilt lever.
- **The mechanical bite:** let the heat reach Gern's parents so they clamp down — grounding literally costs him **Time Points**, making the scapegoat poorer in the currency the episode runs on (debuting the strict-vs-lax-parents TP modifier).
- **The date drops here.** The accusation is *also* how the clock's anchor reaches the players: the smear names *when* Ricky vanished (the dare night / "gone since the weekend"). Player-facing information gained by what happens *to* them, not investigated for.

### Ricky's clock — a *lore* clock, discoverable by investigation
The players must be able to reconstruct "how long does Ricky have" from **facts about Hushes**, not from anything Ricky is doing (they can't see him). The mechanism:
- **What a Hush does with the taken (species fact, discoverable via Russ / the film / a cryptid monograph):** it does *not* kill and eat. It provisions **live prey for its young to learn to hunt on** — and a juvenile can only catch prey that moves and makes noise, so the adult **"ripens"** the prey over days (wears it down toward a panicked, blundering, catchable state). The ripening is a fixed window — **roughly six to seven days from taking to feeding.** That window is the clock.
- **The specific situation (not the film):** the party did *not* kill the juvenile in [[Session 1 — The Dare|Session 1]] — they drove it off **badly wounded**, still discharging spores as it fled the [[The Pillared Reach|Pillared Reach]]. So the ripening is real and it *completes*: the adult is provisioning Ricky as live prey against the day its recovering young is strong enough to hunt again. When that day comes, the young feeds — and Ricky dies. A real, lethal deadline, not a stalled loop.
- **The spore, reframed as a pheromone:** as it fled, the wounded juvenile tagged [[Tank]] with a pheromone marker — a biological *he-hurt-me, get-him.* The adult homes in on that tag: what looks like it **stalking** Tank is the adult hunting the creature that hurt its young. This is *why* it ranges into town away from the den at night — giving the rescue its approach window — and it makes Tank the lure that draws the adult toward the convergence. No sympathy owed and none implied: it's a predator answering its young's distress.
- **The clock is mental math, not forensics.** Two inputs: (1) *the window* — Hushes ripen the taken over ~6–7 days (film / monograph via [[Russ]], or the [[The Library|Library]]'s newspaper pattern); (2) *the anchor date* — surfaced not by investigation but by the smear itself (see *Why the PCs get blamed*), which names when Ricky vanished. Window minus days-elapsed = the deadline. There is **no** read-the-ripening-stage-off-the-evidence step — you adjudicate the countdown, the players do the subtraction.
- **The horror kicker:** the window is the *outer* bound, but nobody knows how fast the wounded juvenile is recovering — so the young could come to feed *early*. The safe read is "get him out well before the window closes," and that uncertainty is scarier than a clean countdown and keeps them from cutting it fine.
- **Design line:** keep the *ripening window* a stable, countable species fact; keep Ricky's personal endurance entirely offstage. You adjudicate "how many days into the window are we," never "is Ricky still holding on."

### Reassembly — the staged opening (the Episode-1 split, honored)
The two dungeon outcomes get different mornings, and the opening is **told out of order** so the lost day is *witnessed,* not narrated:
- **The split.** [[Tank]] & [[Old Man]] fell at the Buzzsaw → **hollowed,** they lose **Day A** to autopilot (DM-controlled). [[Bash Whitlock|Bash]] & [[Gern]] escaped via Wesley's portal → a free **Day A.**
- **Day B — open here.** Run the two fallen kids' confrontations first; each lights the fuse to *find the others:*
  - **[[Tank]] ← [[Dominique Ferraro|Dominique]]:** his crush corners him, furious-because-worried the smear is landing on her cousin Bash — guilt lever; he resolves to make it right with Bash. *(Vector: Tank→Bash.)*
  - **[[Old Man]] ← a neighborhood kid** ("Petey," a younger Flats kid who idolizes him): two payloads — *"you were being weird yesterday"* (the lost day, witnessed from outside) and *"I saw something prowling the streets last night"* (the Hush — the first naming of a monster in town). Old Man becomes the party's first monster-tripwire and, spooked that he lost a day like Tank did, goes to Tank. *(Vector: Old Man→Tank.)* Both are Flats kids a block apart, so they seek each other out or simply **bump into each other** — ideally right after Dominique storms off, two blanked-out kids on one stoop: the nucleus of the party. *(DM irony: the thing the kid saw was casing Tank's block; Old Man carries a warning secretly about the friend he's going to find.)*
- **Day A — flashback ("the day before").** Cut to Bash & Gern's first free day: teach Time Points; run the accusation (above); and have them cross paths with a **placid, hollowed Tank & Old Man** — the cost of falling, witnessed from the outside.
- **Day B — the reconvene.** Four independent vectors make it inevitable: Old Man→Tank, Tank→Bash, Bash→(needs witnesses to the impossible object), Gern→(isolated, the mill kids are the only ones who were there and won't pile on). The **shared-secret click:** Bash & Gern confirm from outside what Tank & Old Man can't remember (*"you two were like zombies yesterday"*), turning *am I insane?* into *this happened to us.* Reward the unit — they arrive holding threads (the monster sighting, the blame, the blank days) they'd never have alone.
- **[[Dominique Ferraro|Dominique]] as connector.** Across it all she's the warm through-line pulling them together out of concern for Bash — the betrayer midwifing the group. **This answers the "make them love Free-Dominique first" TBD in her note.**
- **Supports:** a default hangout ([[Marco's Diner]] front-runner) so "meet me at ___" works; residences (Tank & Old Man are Flats — pin Bash & Gern).

### The mark ([[Tank]])
**Tank carries the mark (locked).** Daytime: low-grade, deniable wrongness — his own name from an empty room, the neighbor's dog that won't settle, a bird dropped dead on the porch — which drives *why me?* [[Old Man]] has already surfaced the monster-in-town idea (see *Reassembly*), so Track B has a running start before the party learns *who* it's circling. Night: the stalk goes active and bridges to the den. Frame the spotlight as a *party-tactics problem*: Tank is bait and liability (can't be the one making noise), so everyone's choices matter. Discovering the mark is on Tank is itself a payoff — he can't hear it; the others can find it.

### The Filter, introduced by texture (not explanation)
First real taste of [[The Reality Filter]], felt before it's understood: adults are *oddly, wrongly accepting.* The sheriff's official line is "wild dog" and won't follow up; the "military school" story is swallowed whole by everyone including teachers; a witness who plainly saw *something* near the mill last week has rationalized it away by morning. The players should feel the fog as an eerie texture — *why is no adult alarmed?* — without being told what it is yet. Pairs with [[Russ]] and [[Wade Vance]] as the two Filter-*resistant* adults they'll meet, which throws the fog into relief. ([[Reverend Hollis]] is a deliberate near-miss: an adult who *is* alarmed — about the wrong thing — see the map note.)

### The climax
Night, the woods, the den. Two threads converge: [[Tank]]'s voice (drawing the adult) and the social hunt for Ricky. **[[Wade Vance]] is already there** — his bond to his son broke the Filter's fog (the way grief broke [[Russ]]; he's the second Filter-resistant adult), and his covert-operator training is why he can track the thing when no adult will. He converges with the PCs and plays a hand in the kill/rescue (role flexes to how they run it), revealing he's far more than the absentee dad the town wrote off (see [[Wade Vance]]). The Hush is killable now that they've researched it — sound-misdirection, split its attention, the film's "silence in a confined space" kill, and the optional stone of quietude if they earned it. **Most of the glory goes to Dad** — so the rescue does *not* publicly ingratiate Ricky with the party, and resolving the disappearance here means it does **not** dangle into the next episode (though the clique's resentment can linger). Ricky is **not hollowed** — the den is a monster's cache, not a dungeon pocket; the abduction is pure terror (total helplessness, the thing he's built himself against), and that helplessness is what later drives him to [[Wesley Crane|Wesley]].

## Ricky's disposition outcomes
Set by **how the rescue goes** (and whether the PCs engage it at all). He'll never thank them publicly — admitting the kids he set up saved him would detonate his image:
- **Clean** (PCs help; Ricky unscathed, Dad unhurt) → a small *private* uptick: no public warmth, but later he won't throw a PC under the bus when he easily could. **2 → 3 (Sympathetic).**
- **Messy** (PCs help but it's rough; **Dad gravely wounded** in the chaos) → Ricky blames the party → **1 (Wary)**, or **0 (Hostile)** if it got ugly. Thematically the worst confirmation for him (chaos broke his order-symbol) — see [[Ricky Vance]] (the lever).
- **Disaster** (egregious botch only) → **Dad dies** → **0 (Hostile)**, a hard accelerant into Wesley. *(Reserve the father's death for a later, higher-stakes beat — the players barely know him yet.)*
- **Disengaged** (the PCs never connect the threads, leaving Dad to it) → stays **2 (Neutral)**; no bond, Wesley works him from scratch.

Naming the prank / dare night as ammo at any point is a pitfall (shame, doubly loaded because it also exposes the fracture inside [[The Party]]). After this episode [[Wesley Crane]] starts paying attention to Ricky and to the engaged PCs; his counter-cultivation begins. See [[The Negotiation System]].

## Aftermath & seeds
- The **adult** Hush's body in dawn light has a **tooth made of something that isn't bone** — dungeon-stone material, a [[Cosmology Seeds|cosmology seed]] to call back in [[Episode 3 — The Click]] (and the recurring-material clue in [[The Pattern Recognition Arc]]). Sheriff's report: "bear."
- **The juvenile is still out there.** It fled Session 1 wounded and never returns to feed — now a loose, half-grown Hush with no adult, a dangling thread to reintroduce later if wanted.
- Ricky comes home **changed and withdrawn** — which only makes the "military school" story feel true to the town.
- A **hooded figure** remains an open question (the slow-burn [[Wesley Crane]] recognition arc) — and [[Reverend Hollis]] has now been *seen circling Wesley* too, a second party the players may misread. Mill-area chalk reads as background ([[Wesley's Sigils|Tally Stone delve]] residue).
- **[[Wade Vance]]** now has a compact encounter statline (his silence is the counter to a sound-hunter) and a Tier-3 covert-unit seed — set up here, payable off later only if the players bite.
