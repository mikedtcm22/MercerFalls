# Player Sourcebook

Up: [[Player-Facing Materials]]

**The player-facing primer: what Mercer Falls is, who lives there, and what *kind* of game this is — texture first, then the three mechanics that set this campaign apart.**

This is reference reading handed to players before play. It teases tone and how-it-plays without spoiling the campaign's secrets. (For the secret layer, see [[Mercer Falls, Ohio]] and [[The Wrongness Layer]] — DM-side.)

## Mercer Falls in '96

A small rust-belt town surviving the loss of its main industry. The [[The Mill|steel mill]] closed in '91 — close to 700 jobs gone in a single summer — and the town hasn't recovered. What keeps the lights on now is tourism: [[Mercer Lake]] and [[The River and the Falls]] draw visitors Memorial Day through Labor Day, then the town goes quiet for a long Ohio winter. It's June 1996. You're playing a kid here.

A quiet, unexplained pattern: visitors rarely come back. Most of the town stopped wondering about it long ago. (Players are not told why — this is a seed.)

## The Town

Built around its lake and river, both running in from the north. Key geography players know:

- **The Lake** — glacial, deep, cold until July; the town's livelihood and scenery in summer.
- **The Mill & the Falls** — the mill sits atop the bluff at town center; the river exits as the falls. From a distance it can look like *the mill is the source of the falls*. People "look past the mill." See [[The Districts]] for the residential areas.
- **Downtown, the Heights, the Flats, the South Side, Sycamore Subdivision, and Out in the County** — the social geography of who lives where (old money, working-class, middle-class, cookie-cutter new builds, rural). Covered in [[The Districts]].

## The People

A gallery of small-town types players have grown up around — Mrs. Brzezinski on her porch, the slow-drawl Sinclair gas attendant (a Filter-touched figure; see [[Henry Voss]]), [[Mike and Doug Tinsmith|Mike Tinsmith]] at the fishing dock, [[Wesley Crane]] who runs the community center, Father Reilly, Mrs. Carney the librarian, Mr. Greaves with the strange garden. The point: the town is small enough that the same faces keep showing up, and *some people you can sense are somewhere else even when they're standing in front of you.*

## How This Campaign Plays

Standard D&D 5e, plus three campaign-specific systems introduced gradually at the table:

1. **Dungeons Are Encounters** — dungeons are antagonists with agendas and moods. Time inside runs on *dungeon turns*; resources and information are earned turn by turn. See [[Dungeoneering Rules]] and [[Dungeon Mechanics]].
2. **Your Time Is Real** — you play a kid with school, parents, chores, curfew. Free time is a spendable resource you commit to specific pursuits. This is the investigation engine. See [[Time Points]].
3. **Conversations Have Stakes** — important social encounters use a structured, multi-turn system where what you've learned about a person matters and you can lose hard-won ground. This is the social character's *combat system*. See [[The Negotiation System]] and [[Negotiation Rules Reference]].

Together these are [[The Two Modes of Play]]: most days you live your kid's life; some days you do something a lot less normal.
