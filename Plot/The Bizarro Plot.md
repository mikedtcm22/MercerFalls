# The Bizarro Plot

Up: [[Plot & Structure]]

**The prep method and the Act 1 shape in one note: in lieu of writing plot, write what happens if the PCs do nothing — that becomes [[Wesley Crane]]'s calendar, and the session is whatever the PCs do to disrupt it.**

> [!danger] DM-only
> This entire note is antagonist machinery. Players experience only the foreground (a monster a week); the structure below is invisible to them.

## The Prep Methodology (bible §18)

- **Write the bizarro plot, not the plot.** Each prep cycle, write the next slice of what the antagonist does absent any PC interference. The session is the PCs' disruption of it. See [[Wesley's Summer Calendar]] for the worked Act-1 example.
- **Failure branches, it doesn't end.** With one exception (the level-20 objective), the PCs failing any objective never ends the campaign — it branches it. The town gets worse; downstream consequences play out; new objectives emerge from the wreckage.
- **Every objective gets two downstreams.** A *success downstream* (whatever the PCs did instead) and a *failure downstream* (the bizarro plot by default), both playable. The canonical example is the Act-1 fork in [[Aftermath Branches]] — and note the gate opens regardless, so both paths still produce a rival party (see [[The Other Party (Rival or Evil)]]), just of different qualities.
- **The town-getting-worse trajectory is gravity; PC action is lift.** They aren't defusing a doomsday clock that explodes on failure. They're making the difference between a town that has a chance and one that doesn't. See [[The Countdown Structure]].
- **Scope of pre-planning:** beat-sheet the full ~14 weeks; fully scene out only the PC-independent events ([[The Equinox Ritual]], both aftermath cases); detail the week-by-week only after PCs exist. Don't pre-plan more than 1–2 sessions ahead.

## Act 1 Overview (bizarro v5 §0–§1)

**Two tracks, two tempos.** [[Wesley Crane]] runs a **town track** (recruits, ritual prep, the public-facing [[The Community Center]]) and a **dungeon track** (sigils, item retrieval, residue-harvesting) — he is the dungeon's user. He has all summer, so his moves are spread out, deniable, hard to flag without context. The PCs' tempo is the opposite: **episode-driven**, a new monster problem every week or two needing town investigation and dungeon resolution. They won't initially read these as connected, let alone as Wesley's fault.

**The three-phase realization arc.** The Wesley reveal isn't a single moment; it's the cumulative result of 4–6 episodes' details lining up. See [[The Pattern Recognition Arc]] for the DM tracking detail.

- **Phase 1 (Ep1–2):** "This town has weird problems." PCs solve monsters; don't ask why yet.
- **Phase 2 (Ep3–5):** "Something is behind this." The problems share a source — the dungeon is leaking. *First click.*
- **Phase 3 (Ep6–8):** "It's that guy at the community center." Wesley's name accumulates; a faceup conversation confirms it. *Second click.*

The DM plants connections densely enough that an attentive party gets there, with NPC nudges held ready — without telegraphing the phases or being coy.

**MOTW episode structure.** Each episode = 1–2 sessions of ~1.5 hr: **Hook** (10 min, in medias res, the problem already happening) → **Town investigation** (30–40 min, 1–2 days of [[Time Points]], divided labor) → **Resolution** (35–45 min, a delve, a town confrontation, or a hybrid) → **Aftermath** (5–10 min, a lingering thread). Default is hybrid; vary the mode mix to keep the formula fresh. A two-session episode splits at the cliffhanger between "we know what/where it is" and "we go in tomorrow." See [[Episodes]].
