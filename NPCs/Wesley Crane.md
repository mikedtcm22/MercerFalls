# Wesley Crane

Up: [[Cast & NPCs]]

**Charismatic transplant who runs the [[The Community Center]] grief-support scene, genuinely well-liked — and the dungeon's current Order-side operator, being consumed exactly as the man before him was: the savior who never needed saving.**

> [!danger] DM-only
> Nearly everything below is secret. The town sees a warm, kindly preservationist. The PCs spend most of Act 1 not even suspecting him; the slow confirmation is the spine of the campaign's antagonist reveal (Episodes 6–8).

## Profile
~30s, in Mercer Falls about five years. Runs the spiritual community center, leads grief groups, gave the eulogy at a beloved teacher's funeral. Genuinely warm, genuinely well-liked. Recruits troubled-but-talented locals (see [[Wesley's Recruits]]). He came deliberately: working in an inner city before this, he chased an obscure footnote to the **Mercer papers** ([[Elias Mercer]]'s legacy) — a thread most would have glossed over — and in the fragments he scraped together he saw the potential for *a method to make people better.* He left almost immediately and has given his life to finishing what he believes Mercer started, performing an entry ritual that gave him contact and protection. **He believes he is using a tool. He is the tool.**

## Who he actually is — the savior who never needed saving
The key to Wesley: **he has never been broken.** He's spent his whole life on the *outside* of suffering — competent, clear-eyed, never himself addicted or grieving or lost — looking in at people who are. What he's actually felt across all those years isn't empathy; it's **frustration.** He sees, with real and maddening clarity, the obvious thing a person should do — and watches them not do it, and spiral, again and again. The successes kept him going; the accumulating failures wore him toward hopelessness. The Mercer papers didn't offer him healing (he was never sick) — they offered **leverage:** a way to make people better whether they cooperate or not.

So his sincerity is genuine *as behavior* and hollow *at the root.* He loves the **idea** of helping more than he has ever loved an actual person. Underneath the warmth sits a quiet contempt he experiences as compassion: *most people are hopeless on their own and need someone to fix them — and he's up to the task.* This is why he's [[Order and Chaos|Order]]'s perfect vessel before he ever touches the dungeon — **he already believes a person's own agency is the broken thing, the part that needs overriding for their own good.** The dungeon doesn't corrupt him into that view. It rewards a view he walked in with, and finishes a sentence he'd already started.

> [!danger] He never knows any of this is wrong.
> The contempt is **invisible to him.** He experiences every refusal to let someone go as devotion, and would be genuinely wounded to hear his care named as control. He is not *performing* sincerity — he believes it utterly, which is exactly why the town can't see through him and the PCs spend Act 1 unable to suspect him.

## The gospel
What he *says* is impeccable, and kind: *"You're not broken — you're stuck, and stuck is solvable. You don't have to white-knuckle this alone. There's a way through, and people who've walked it, and a place for you here while you do."* Everything a drowning person aches to hear. What he *believes*, and never says, is the premise underneath: people can't be trusted to save themselves, so the merciful thing is to **take the choosing off their hands.** A real counselor says *"I'll help you find your way."* Wesley says *"I'll show you the way"* — and that one word is the whole horror. It's pure [[Order and Chaos|Order]]: **relief from the burden of choosing, sold as mercy.** Grief-support is flawless cover, because grief is the one pain where *"there was nothing you could have done / just follow the steps / let us carry it"* is legitimately, decently comforting — so his actual worldview hides inside genuinely good bereavement care.

## How he seduces — diagnosis as intimacy
His signature move is **being seen.** Seeing people clearly is his real gift, and being seen *that accurately* is intoxicating to anyone who feels invisible or out of control. He names your problem better than you can, names the way out, and the precision lands like love. (It isn't; it's the same clarity that frustrated him for twenty years, finally meeting people who'll let him drive.) The conversion runs **be seen → be relieved → be steered.** Mannerisms for the table:
- **A brilliant listener with a clock running.** He absorbs everything and reflects it back sharper than you said it — but there's a faint impatience under the warmth, a sense he already knows where this goes and is waiting for you to catch up. A sharp PC can clock that the "listening" is really *waiting.*
- **He never argues; he reframes.** He won't tell you you're wrong — he'll tell you what your pain *really* means, and his meaning always hands him the wheel.
- **Generous with relief, stingy with autonomy.** He'll do enormous things *for* you. He will not let you do them *yourself.*
- **The hollowing tell:** as he's consumed, the warmth gets *efficient* — the same lines, delivered faster, because the person matters less and the result matters as much. Early Wesley lingers; late Wesley doesn't.

## The cast and the permanent cast
The keystone of the character, and the reason he isn't a strawman: **he is sometimes right — about the cast, never about its duration.** A temporary surrender of agency — letting someone carry you while you can't carry yourself — is real, good, sometimes necessary: a *cast on a broken leg.* Wesley has genuinely seen it work. But he drew exactly the wrong lesson from his own successes. The relief came from the person being given **room to take their own wheel back;** he reads it as proof that *him taking the wheel* is the cure. He mistakes the scaffold for the building. His "failures" are often just people he wouldn't let *out* of the cast; his successes are people who healed and walked away — which he reads as the cast working, not as proof they no longer needed him. It's a **truth held by the wrong end:** right that people sometimes need to surrender the wheel, wrong that the surrender is the destination rather than the detour. That is the [[Order and Chaos|Order]] error at human scale — *help that refuses to end is control wearing help's face.* See **[[The Cast and the Permanent Cast]]** for the full principle and how it generates each Rival's lever.

## Who he recruits — and the lever on each
He hunts one shape: **a gifted kid whose own choices are visibly failing them, who is starting to suspect they can't be trusted to run their own life** — the one most ready to hand the wheel over with gratitude. Three filters:
1. **A real gift** — he's drawn to talent (wasted potential is the exact frustration that wore him down; and the ritual needs capable witnesses).
2. **A wound he reads as self-inflicted** — the kid is stuck or spiraling in a way he can frame as *"you keep choosing this."* (Often unfair — that read is the contempt showing.)
3. **Isolation** — no one else is offering to carry it, so his offer is the only one on the table.

This filter generates both halves of every Rival (see [[Wesley's Recruits]] and [[The Cast and the Permanent Cast]]):
- **The pressure-point Wesley leans on** = the place the kid secretly agrees with him — *"I can't trust myself; just tell me what to do."* Their specific **hand-over-the-wheel** moment.
- **The saving-grace the party plays to** = the inverse — the kid's own buried agency, the choice that's genuinely theirs to trust. The party **cannot out-care Wesley** (he out-cares everyone, lavishly); the one thing he structurally *cannot* offer is **help aimed at the kid not needing him** — peer-trust, treating them as someone who can be trusted to choose, knowing when the cast comes off. That's why acknowledgment-as-an-equal reaches them, and why Wesley counter-cultivates by reframing PC respect as condescension: peer-trust is the single move that breaks his pitch. The danger stays real — trusting a not-quite-healed kid to walk can mean watching them fall.

## The dungeon's user
Wesley is Order's current operator of the containment system. Without a user the dungeon is a static deathtrap; with Wesley it adapts, resets, reshuffles, and pressures intruders. Most of what PCs read as the dungeon "thinking" is Wesley operating it through escalating [[Wesley's Sigils]] granted by his anti-ember relic. **Wesley's progression and the dungeon's escalation are the same thing** — the principal axis of antagonist scaling (see [[Dungeon Mechanics]]). Damage him — clear his sigils, kill [[Wesley's Adherents]], exhaust him — and the dungeon feels less alive: floors stay cleared, resets slip. [[Elias Mercer]] ran it before him (DM-private previous-user thread).

## The relic (anti-ember)
An artifact he extracted early; the prototype of what the campaign calls **anti-embers** (parallel to the PCs' [[The Embers]] but dungeon/Order-side). Fire-vocabulary: *steady, banked, even — a coal burning a long time without consuming itself.*
- **In-dungeon hollowing protection:** the relic absorbs the whole transaction — no dissolution, no reconstitution, no lost day, no residue. Superior to the embers, but the cost is paid *from Wesley himself*.
- **His agency-loss is inverted.** The town's hollowing is apathy; Wesley's is the opposite — more focused, more certain, radiant after a delve that should have wrecked him. There is less of him each time, but what remains is harder, narrower, committed. Warmth toward people becomes warmth toward The Work. *(A man whose warmth was always slightly performed has the least to lose in the trade — the dungeon barely has to change him.)*
- **Outside the dungeon, no protection.** PCs can kill him in the real world.

## Sigils (mechanical aside)
The relic grants sigil-class abilities that scale as he integrates further (i.e., is consumed). PCs cannot use sigils (relic is prerequisite; a captured notebook is inert in their hands). See [[Wesley's Sigils]] for full mechanics. Level 1 **Real Sigils** active from Session 1; Level 2 **Decoy Sigils** unlock mid-Act-1. Clearing a real sigil costs him significant delve progress — one of the highest-leverage PC moves.

## Long arc: Order's user → Chaos's champion
- **Phase 1 (Sessions 1–late Act 2): Order's User.** Power/knowledge-hungry; building a clockwork community via the community center; recruits are parts in his structure, valued but not as individuals.
- **The Break (late Act 2):** a [[The Mercer Mansion]] document (the Mercer papers) makes the previous-user truth unavoidable. He sees his own future in [[Elias Mercer]]'s. There isn't enough self left to recover.
- **Phase 2 (Act 3): Chaos's Champion.** The same hollowed zeal swings hard toward Chaos — and it's the **same cast-error scaled to apocalypse.** Having concluded humanity can't be trusted to walk, he stops offering casts and reaches for *amputation:* tear the containment down, break the whole structure of choice, let what's inside *out.* The [[The Entity]] recognizes him as an asset. **Combat-Wesley scales up brutally; identity-Wesley scales down.**
- **The Act-3 vulnerability (the "him").** By the end the path to defeating him may run not through the entity he's becoming but through **who he used to be** — and the specific crack is the memory his whole life is organized around *not* seeing: **someone he once "saved" who got better and *left*** — proof the cast was always meant to come off. Not an argument; a memory. (Identity TBD; ties to his pre-Mercer-Falls inner-city years — see [[The Cast and the Permanent Cast]].)

## Act 1 plan & the equinox
His objective is **opening [[The Mercer Gate]]** via the autumn-equinox ritual (Sunday, Sept 22). The lock needs three elements: dungeon materials ([[The Three Ritual Items]]), a Mercer bloodline relative ([[Sam]]), and four hollowed-but-conscious witnesses (the recruits). Anti-embers for the witnesses are a *side-effect*, not the purpose. **The gate opens whether Wesley lives or dies** — kill him early and [[Sam]] completes what's left. See [[The Equinox Ritual]], [[Aftermath Branches]], [[Where the PCs Can Disrupt]].

He also pulls [[The Keystone Theft]] around the Floor 3 window — confirming he's been watching the PCs and locking his identity to the chalk-cartography style.

## At the equinox negotiation
**Wesley is not negotiable** — too hollowed to reach. Treated as Hostile (Interest 1, Patience 1), no real motivations to hit. His role is the antagonist running the opposing campaign: counter-arguing the Rivals, progressing the Ritual Progress Track, occasionally attacking. The obstacle, not the target. See [[The Negotiation System]].
