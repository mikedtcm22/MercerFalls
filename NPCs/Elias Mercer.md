# Elias Mercer

Up: [[Cast & NPCs]]

**Founder of [[Mercer Falls, Ohio]] (1887), occult-library industrialist — and, in the campaign's hidden history, the dungeon's *previous* user, consumed by it exactly as [[Wesley Crane]] is being consumed now.**

> [!danger] DM-only
> Mercer-as-previous-user is one of the campaign's most important DM-private threads. To the town and the [[Mercer Falls Historical Society]] he is simply the founder. The payoff is that his papers are *Wesley's own future, written in advance.*

## Profile
Industrialist who founded the town in 1887; kept a private library of occult texts; member of a small, persistent gentleman's club whose attenuated descendants still exist in town (see [[The Mercer Gentleman's Club]]; [[Sam]] is canonically a Mercer descendant). Direct Mercer line is extinct — the last descendant died in the early 1970s and willed the house to the [[Mercer Falls Historical Society]].

## Function in the campaign
- **The dungeon's previous user.** Mercer found the dungeon in the 1880s, **built [[The Mill]] on top of its south entrance as cover** for his real work, and operated it for decades while the town grew around him. He was consumed by it — a man becoming a vessel for Order without realizing it.
- **He sealed [[The Mercer Gate]] in 1887.** Opening it is the entire point of [[The Equinox Ritual]].
- **The Mercer papers.** He left journals, ledgers, and occult-library marginalia documenting the full trajectory of his hollowing. [[Wesley Crane]] read them in his early years in town and used them to find the dungeon — without yet reading them *as his own future*. They are sealed in the locked inner archive of [[The Mercer Mansion]]'s library annex (keyholders: [[Mrs. Devereaux]], the board chair, and Wesley). They are the campaign's answer to "what is Wesley going to become," delivered as historical document, and most likely what triggers **Wesley's Break** in late Act 2 — and one of the only non-combat weapons the PCs have against him. See [[Wesley Crane]], [[Order and Chaos]].
